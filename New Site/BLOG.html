A Typical Night on the "Hill"

Monday, a quiet day over the festive season period at Van Reenens Pass on the N3, a welcome respite after Sunday morning's horror crash outside Harrismith which claimed thirty lives, ten of them children. A day where vehicles and equipment are washed and checked and medical consumables are replaced in each vehicle, a period where the members of the Community Medical Services Team can relax and spend some healing time after this recent tragedy.

18:35 – A call comes through advising of an obstruction between ourselves and Montrose, approximately 6kms north of our base. A vehicle is despatched to assist with scene safety.

On arrival, we find a heavy truck that has damaged its propshaft, and is obstructing the slow northbound lane. A call is placed to the operator in Johannesburg to advise him of the trucks situation. Now normally the sequence of events in a case like this, would be a call to the operator to advise him of the situation, a call to a towing service to remove the vehicle to a safe position, thereby opening the road and removing a potentially dangerous obstruction, and releasing those emergency personnel that are assisting. However in this case, the operator refuses to accept liability for any towing costs, and instead despatches another horse from Johannesburg. This horse eventually arrives around 01:00 on Tuesday morning. In the meantime, the 'traditional' mist had covered the entire area, and attempts to manage and maintain an element of safety had become dangerously difficult. Apart from the lives of those tasked with protecting the scene, the chances of secondary crashes became a real issue as visibility reduced to less than 30 metres.

The Saga Continues

Whilst we all accept the economics and increasing costs of operating a transport and distribution company, what happened here in our opinion was unacceptable.

In KwaZulu Natal, south of our position, there is no option – if a vehicle is causing an obstruction and cannot be repaired within approximately 30 minutes, a tow truck is authorised by the Road Traffic Inspectorate, the vehicle is removed and the costs are forwarded to the operator. Personally, I believe this to be the correct and most efficient method of dealing with such a situation – lives and safety come first !!!

05:10 – After a couple of hours sleep, another call for an obstruction to the northbound slow lane some 20 kms north of our position. A vehicle is despatched in thick mist to assist.

On arrival, we're greeted by two tired N3 Toll Concessions patrol personnel who are already on scene, who advise us that this heavy truck has run out of diesel. Once again, a dangerous situation has now developed, as at this time of the morning, large volumes of trucks are once again on the move, and with the mist reducing visibility once again to around 30 metres, combined with some drivers that are not yet fully awake, our lives and those of other road users becomes seriously prejudiced. What was incredibly frustrating, was that this driver had passed a fuel station less than 5kms previously !!!

The N3 Toll Concessions patrol vehicle, assists by taking the driver to purchase 40 litres of diesel, sufficient to get the vehicle to Harrismith. The patrol vehicle returned with the diesel, and attempts to restart the truck were initiated. Fortunately, at this point, the mist had started to dissipate, and visibility was improving.

Next minute, the terrifying sound of tyres squealing, we all swung around and about 200 metres away, a heavy truck travelling south had lost control and was in the process of rolling. The end result – one person killed and one critically injured, one destroyed truck, trailer and contents and more devastated family members of those killed and injured.

What caused this particular accident – we can only guess, however a combination of speed and fatigue would be my guess.

Statistically there is a marked increase in the number of truck accidents that take place early in the morning, and from 29 years of personal observations on the N3 over peak traffic periods, fatigue has got to be a major role player in these crashes.

Why can we not increase tolls by a few cents, and allow access by truckers to free truck stops that offer security, a safe place to sleep, decent ablutions and good meals. Those hundreds of truckers who choose not to utilise the current truck stops and who park on the side of the road, not only are illegal and create a dangerous environment, but also run the risk of losing wheels and whatever product they may be transporting. It must be impossible to have a good night's sleep, when you have to keep one ear open for criminals that may be tampering with your vehicle!
